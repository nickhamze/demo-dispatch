A postcard is the slowest message worth sending. There are slower messages -
a letter is slower, a telegram, in the few corners of the world where it
still exists, would be even slower if it were not also extinct - but the
postcard is the slowest message that anyone now routinely sends. You write
it sitting at a small café table, you carry it in your bag for a day or
two, you find a postbox, you drop it in. Two weeks later it arrives in
somebody else's letterbox. By then, you are home.

This is the entire point.

A text message arrives instantly and conveys, on average, almost nothing.
"Hi." "Wish you were here." "Got home safe." A postcard arrives slowly and
also conveys almost nothing - the same "wish you were here," the same
weather report - but the slowness changes the meaning. A postcard is not a
message. A postcard is a small physical object that someone you know
selected, carried, wrote on, addressed, and posted, on a specific day, in a
specific place. The text is almost beside the point. The text is what is
written on the back of the gift.

## Cards as objects

I keep all my postcards. There are not many - perhaps two or three a year
arrive at my flat - but each one stays. They live in a small wooden box on
the mantelpiece, in no particular order, and once or twice a year I take
them out and read them and put them back.

The text on most of the cards is unmemorable. A friend's holiday in
Norfolk. Another friend's bad weather in the Lake District. A
half-illegible note from someone I have not seen in three years. The point
of the box is not the text. The point of the box is that all of these
people, on a specific morning, in a specific town, decided I was worth a
small piece of paper and a postage stamp.

## What gets lost in the post

Royal Mail loses a small percentage of postcards. So does every other
postal service. There is no return address on most postcards, so a lost
postcard is not returned to the sender; it is, eventually, destroyed.

I find this enormously moving. Somebody chose the card, paid for the card,
wrote on the card, addressed it, posted it, and then it never arrived. The
recipient does not know, because they never expected it. The sender, after
a few months, suspects, but cannot be sure - perhaps the friend just hasn't
mentioned it. The card sits in a sorting office somewhere, then in a bin,
then in landfill. The kind thought it represented exists, has always
existed, but is now invisible to everyone except the person who had it.

Almost every kind thought, in the entire history of the world, has been like
this. The postcard makes the everyday case visible.

## Buy six and write them all today

If you are travelling: buy six postcards from the first newsagent you find.
Write them, all of them, that evening. Address them. Post them in the
morning. You will pay slightly less than you would for one of those small,
disappointing meals you do not really want anyway. The cards will arrive,
weeks later, scattered across whatever week your friends are having - none
of them will arrive on the same day. Each of them will be small, and brief,
and not quite enough.

That is, I think, exactly what they are for.
