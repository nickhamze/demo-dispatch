Blue, as a colour, has had a very strange career. It is the colour of the
sky, which is the largest object most of us see in any given day, and it is
the colour of the sea, which is the second largest. And yet, for most of
human history, it has been almost impossible to make.

The first useful blue pigment in the western world was lapis lazuli, ground
from a stone mined almost exclusively in what is now Afghanistan. By the
time it reached a European studio in the fifteenth century, it cost more by
weight than gold. Painters used it sparingly, sometimes negotiating with
their patrons over how many square inches of robe could be that particular,
extravagant ultramarine. Mary's robes, in many altarpieces, are blue not
because someone decided blue was the right colour for Mary but because blue
was the most expensive thing the workshop could afford to put on the panel,
and Mary deserved it.

## A long absence

Languages, on the whole, did not have a word for blue until quite late in
their development. Homer described the sea as "wine-dark." Ancient Hebrew,
ancient Chinese, ancient Japanese all routinely grouped together what we
would now call blue and green, or blue and grey, or sometimes blue and
black. This is not because their speakers could not see the colour. It is
because the colour was not, for most purposes, distinct enough from its
neighbours to need its own word. You named the colours that you needed to
name. You needed to name red, because red was blood and red was warning. You
did not need to name blue, because nothing useful in your immediate
environment was decisively, only blue.

The Egyptians were an exception. They synthesised a blue pigment from
copper, sand, and lime - the famous Egyptian blue - and used it everywhere.
Their tomb paintings glow with it. The technique was lost after Rome fell,
and Europe spent more than a thousand years going back to grinding stones.

## Industrial blue

The breakthrough was, as is often the case, an accident. In 1704 a German
chemist named Heinrich Diesbach was trying to make a red pigment. He failed.
What he made instead was a deep, slightly purple blue, almost as good as
ultramarine and almost as cheap as dirt. Prussian blue, as it came to be
called, made blue available to ordinary printers and ordinary painters for
the first time.

Hokusai used it. The great wave is Prussian blue. Vermeer's late paintings
are full of it. The blueprint, that nineteenth-century technical drawing
that copied a master onto blue-tinted paper, is Prussian blue. Once you can
make a blue cheaply, blue stops being a luxury and becomes a default. By the
1860s, blue was, for the first time in human history, ordinary.

## Klein

In 1956 a French painter named Yves Klein started making paintings that were
nothing but blue. One canvas. One colour. He called the particular shade he
used International Klein Blue, and he eventually patented it, though the
patent covered the binder rather than the pigment itself. The pigment was a
synthetic ultramarine that had been available for decades. Klein's
contribution was insisting that you look at the colour without anything else
to look at.

It is, I think, the right way to end a brief history of blue. For thousands
of years humans worked very hard to put a small amount of blue on something
else - on a wall, in a manuscript, on a panel of a saint. And then
eventually somebody made a painting that was just blue. There was nothing
else in the frame. The work was the colour.

I think about this when I open a fresh bottle of ink. The ink is, almost
always, a particular shade of blue-black. It is cheaper than I can imagine.
It pours the same as it would have poured in any century. And the shade
inside the bottle is darker, deeper, and more interesting than any single
material the western world had access to before about 1700.

It is, every time, slightly miraculous.
