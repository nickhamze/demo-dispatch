The observatory is on a hilltop about forty minutes' drive from the
nearest town, on a piece of land that was donated to a local astronomy
society in the 1970s by a farmer who, depending on which version of the
story you believe, either believed in their work or just did not want the
land any more.

The dome is small. The telescope inside it is small. The members of the
society, on the night I visited, numbered four. They had set out three
folding chairs at the back of the dome, a flask of tea, and a plate of
biscuits.

![A small refracting telescope on its tripod, viewed in low light.](telescope.webp)

The thing being observed, that night, was Saturn. The rings were sharp.
The moons - I counted four, though there were probably more present - sat
in a casual diagonal across the field of view. The other members took
turns at the eyepiece. Each of them murmured "still there" or "still
beautiful" or, in one case, "still impossible," and then stepped back to
let the next person look.

I think this is what I want to record about the visit. There was no
discovery being made. There was no programme of observations. There was
no data being collected. Four people had driven up a hill, on a Tuesday
evening, in indifferent weather, to look at a planet that they had all
looked at many times before, that has not changed in any human sense in
hundreds of millions of years, and that will be exactly the same the next
time they look at it. They were, in the most ordinary sense, paying
attention.

I have been thinking about this for several months. I do not have anything
clever to add. The biscuits, in case you were wondering, were shortbread.
