The pier is short - perhaps two hundred metres, end to end - and on most
weekday evenings there is nobody on it but a handful of fishermen and one
or two people walking dogs. The fishermen do not, in my observation, catch
much. The dogs are interested in the smells and not in the water.

I came down to watch the boats come in. There were three boats in the bay
when I arrived, sails up, tacking towards the harbour entrance in the kind
of slow, thoughtful zigzag that a sailing boat makes when the wind is
light and behind it. The lead boat made the entrance at about half past
seven. The second boat followed about ten minutes later. The third boat
gave up, dropped anchor, and decided to wait until morning.

I have been thinking about the third boat ever since. It is a hard thing
to do, knowing the harbour is right there, and to choose instead to spend
the night on the open water. There must be reasons. None of them are
visible from a pier.

By eight o'clock the harbour was full and the light had drained out of the
sky. I walked back along the pier in the half-dark. The fishermen had
packed up. The dogs had gone home.

It was, all in all, a successful evening.
