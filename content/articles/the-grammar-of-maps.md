A map is, on the face of it, a picture of a place. This is misleading.
A map is an argument about a place. The cartographer chooses what to put in
and what to leave out, what to enlarge and what to suppress, what to colour
red and what to render in faint grey. The choices are not innocent. They
add up to a sentence the map is making about the territory.

The most interesting things about a map are the things it has decided not to
show.

## Generalisation

The technical term is *generalisation*. A real coastline, viewed from a
satellite, is a fractal mess of inlets and small headlands. A map of that
coast at 1:50,000 simplifies it - it draws a smooth curve where there are
in fact a thousand small wiggles. At 1:250,000 the curve gets even smoother;
many small bays disappear entirely. At 1:1,000,000 the coast becomes a few
sweeping lines.

You cannot make a useful map without generalising. The exact shape of the
shore changes every time the tide comes in, anyway. But each level of
generalisation makes a choice. A particular small cove might still appear
on the 1:50,000 map and have vanished on the 1:250,000. The cartographer
decided it was important enough at one scale and not important enough at
another. Probably nobody asked the people who lived in the cove.

## What the colours mean

Open any modern road atlas. The motorways are red or blue, depending on the
country. The major roads are yellow or orange. The minor roads are white,
or thin black lines, or in some atlases not shown at all. Settlements above
a certain population threshold are named in larger type than settlements
below it. National borders are heavier than regional borders. Rivers are
blue, forests are green, marshland is a kind of stippled green.

These are conventions, learned in childhood, that we no longer notice.
They were not inevitable. The Ordnance Survey colours one set of maps in a
particular way. Soviet military maps used a different palette and would
have looked, to a British navigator of the same era, faintly alien. The map
is in a language; you have to learn the language to read it.

## What the projection commits to

Any flat map of a round world is a lie, the question is just which lie.
Mercator preserves angles, which is useful for sailing in a straight line,
but it makes Greenland look the size of Africa. Peters preserves area, which
is useful for political arguments, but the shapes are absurd - South
America turns into a sausage. The polar stereographic preserves shapes near
the pole at the cost of ridiculously enlarging the equator.

There is no neutral projection. Every map of the world has decided which of
its lies it is willing to tell. Mercator's shape-preservation made it the
default for most of the European colonial era, partly because the same
property that made it useful for sailors also made it convenient for
empires - it makes the temperate north look bigger and the tropical south
look smaller. The convenience and the ideology are not unrelated.

## What an honest map omits

The honest map admits its omissions. The little legend in the corner says
*scale 1:50,000* and *contour interval 10m* and *projection: transverse
Mercator*. It says, in effect, here is what I am pretending to be a picture
of, and here is roughly how much I have flattened, smoothed, recoloured,
and dropped. A map without a scale bar, without a north arrow, without a
projection note, is hiding things. Sometimes it is hiding things on
purpose.

The map you draw on the back of a napkin to explain to a friend how to find
your house is also hiding things. It does not bother with a scale bar
because it does not need one - both you and your friend know it is not to
scale. It does not bother with a north arrow because, again, you both know
which way north is. It is honest in the way a child's drawing is honest: it
shows the things that matter and ignores the things that do not. There are
worse models for cartography.
