There is a fern on the kitchen windowsill that produces, every spring, a
single new frond. The frond emerges, in late February or early March, as
a small tight spiral - what botanists call a fiddlehead, after the curled
top of a violin - and over the course of about six weeks it slowly
uncurls into a flat, feathered green leaf that is roughly the size of my
hand.

The uncurling is, on any given day, invisible. You can sit and watch the
fiddlehead for an hour and nothing will happen. But if you take a
photograph of it on Monday and another photograph on Friday, the
difference is unmistakable. The spiral has loosened. The pale green has
darkened. The whole structure has rotated slightly to face the window.

This is the seventh year I have lived with this fern. Each year I tell
myself I am going to take a photograph every day of the uncurling, end to
end, and assemble them into a small flipbook. Each year I forget after
about the third day. The fern continues to uncurl regardless.

This year, again, I am going to take a photograph every day. We will see
how it goes.
