Weather vanes mostly take the same shape - a horizontal arrow that swings
on a vertical pin until the front of the arrow points into the wind. The
back of the arrow, conventionally, is enlarged into a fin that catches the
breeze and turns the whole thing around. Below the spinning part there are
usually four fixed letters - **N**, **S**, **E**, **W** - which let you
read the direction the arrow is pointing in.

[gallery columns="2" size="medium" link="file"]

The vane on the chapel at the corner of my street is a plain arrow, no
ornament. The vane on the boathouse two streets over is a fish - the head
points into the wind, the tail acts as the fin. The vane on the old
firehouse, which is now a bookshop, is a horse galloping. The horse always
points east in the morning and west in the late afternoon, because the
prevailing winds in this part of the world are not particularly strong
and the building catches its own breeze on the side that is in the sun.

The vane I look at most often is on a building I cannot identify. It is
very high up, on a tower that may or may not belong to a school, and it
has the shape of a bird with very long wings. From street level you cannot
tell whether the bird is supposed to be a swallow, a heron, or a stork. It
moves slowly, even on windy days, and on still days it sits in whatever
direction it was last pointing, which means there is no useful information
to be read from it. I look at it anyway.

There is, somewhere, a person whose job it is to maintain weather vanes -
to climb up and oil the pivot, to repaint the letters, to bend the fin
back into shape after a storm. I have never met them. I do not know whether
they belong to a single trade or whether the work is split between
roofers, sign painters, and steeplejacks. The vanes are still spinning, in
any case, which means somebody must be doing it.

This is, on balance, the thing I like most about weather vanes. They are
maintained by hands you never see, in service of information you can read
without having to think, in directions that have not changed in any human
sense for thousands of years. The wind is always the same wind. The vanes
are always the same shape. Somebody, somewhere, is climbing up the steeple
in the rain.
