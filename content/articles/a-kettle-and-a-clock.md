Our kettle takes exactly four minutes and eleven seconds to boil from cold.
I know this because I have, over many mornings, timed it. The kettle does
not know it. The kettle is a perfectly ordinary stovetop kettle, in our
case enamel, with a small whistle in the spout that makes a noise of about
two seconds' duration once the water is at a full rolling boil.

This means our kitchen has, every morning, a soft alarm clock that goes
off four minutes and eleven seconds after we turn the gas on. It is the
sound, more than any other, that wakes the household. It is also, by some
distance, the most reliable timekeeper in the house. Our actual clocks all
disagree with each other by a minute or two. The kettle agrees with the
sun.

I find this enormously comforting. The kettle does not know what time it
is. It does not know what day it is. It does not know whether anybody is
awake to hear it. It just boils for four minutes and eleven seconds, every
morning, and announces that it has finished.

It is a small thing, and it has been the same small thing for the eleven
years we have lived in this kitchen, and it will probably continue to be
the same small thing for whatever time we have left here. The kettle is
older than my marriage, older than our cat, older than the table the
kettle sits on top of. It has not changed. It will not change.

I am not making a larger point. I am only saying that the kettle is, in
its particular way, a clock.
