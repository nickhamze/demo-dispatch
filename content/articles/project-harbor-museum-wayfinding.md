**Client:** A one-room museum at the end of the breakwater. Forty exhibits,
one volunteer at the door, no signage budget for fifteen years.

**Brief:** A small, durable wayfinding system that fits the space. Cards
visitors can carry, a single exterior sign, and a folded plan of the room.
Read at a glance by anyone walking in cold.

**Approach.** We spent a Saturday in the museum watching how people moved
through it. They moved badly, the way people do in any one-room museum, in
loops, missing the case in the far corner with the brass log every time.

![A small brass compass, the needle pointing north.](compass.webp)

We made three things.

First, a folded plan of the room, A5 closed and A2 open, with the forty
exhibits numbered in the order the volunteers recommend you see them. One
edge of the plan is a small narrative — the history of the breakwater in
six paragraphs.

Second, a deck of forty pocket cards, one per exhibit, in two colors. The
front of each card is the exhibit's name, number, and date. The back is a
single paragraph that tells you what to look for. The cards live in a small
wooden tray near the door.

![The harbor seen from the breakwater at low tide.](harbor.webp)

Third, an exterior sign. Cast aluminum, two-color enamel, mounted at eye
height on the seaward side of the building so it catches the light from the
afternoon. It says simply: HARBOR MUSEUM, OPEN.

**Outcome.** Visits up forty percent in the first month, according to the
volunteer at the door, who is keeping a tally on a postcard taped above
the cash box. People are now finding the brass log in the corner.
