Twelve pens, photographed at a small north-facing window over the course of
a quiet afternoon. None of them is mine. All of them belong to a friend
whose name I have asked to keep out of the writeup.

[gallery columns="3" size="medium" link="file"]

The first pen, a steel-nibbed school pen of the kind you can still buy in
any stationer for the price of a cup of coffee. The nib is fine. The grip
is plastic. It writes a thin, slightly scratchy line, and the ink dries on
the page within a few seconds. It is the cheapest pen in the room and one
of the most pleasant to use.

The second is a much older pen, a piston-filler from the 1950s with a gold
nib. The nib has been sprung at some point in its history and now writes
with a soft give that makes every letter feel as though it is being placed
deliberately. The ink dries slowly. The line varies with the angle.

The third pen has not written in a long time. The ink in the converter has
dried into a small block of pigment. We will leave it where it is.

The fourth, fifth and sixth pens are all the same model - a modern Japanese
pen sold in three colours, with three matching converters and three matching
boxes. My friend bought all three on a single afternoon, in a single shop,
in a city that has very good pen shops. None of them has ever leaked. The
nibs are interchangeable.

The seventh pen is a calligraphy dip pen, which is not strictly a fountain
pen at all. We are including it because the holder is beautiful.

The eighth pen is plastic and forgettable.

The ninth, tenth and eleventh pens are all variants on a single design - a
cigar-shaped resin barrel with a steel-and-gold two-tone nib - in three
colours that the manufacturer no longer makes. They write almost identically.
The colour is the only meaningful difference between them.

The twelfth pen is the one my friend uses every day. It is a forty-year-old
pen with a slightly bent nib, a barrel that has been polished smooth by
decades of being in and out of pockets, and a converter that needs to be
refilled approximately once a fortnight. It writes a steady, medium line in
a colour my friend mixes themselves from two bottles of commercial ink.
Asked to choose one pen out of the twelve to keep, my friend would not
hesitate.

This is, on balance, how it usually goes. You can have any number of pens.
You will use one of them.
