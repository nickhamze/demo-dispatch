The library on the corner has one tall window that catches the afternoon
sun, and on the wide sill of that window the librarians have, over many
decades, built up a small museum.

![A bookshelf seen from the library window seat.](bookshelf.webp)

Today, on the sill, in roughly the order I notice them:

- A glass paperweight, about the size of a small apple, with a single
  pressed flower inside it. The flower is a daisy, mostly white, with the
  yellow centre faded to a kind of soft cream.
- Three glass marbles, very ordinary, of the kind a child would carry in
  a pocket.
- A small bronze figurine of a tortoise, about three centimetres long,
  worn smooth by being picked up.
- A library card belonging to a previous reader, folded in quarters and
  used as a bookmark in a book that has long since been returned without
  it. The card lists thirty-eight checkouts. The dates are from the late
  1970s.
- A letter opener with a horn handle, blunted by use.
- A small ceramic dish containing five paperclips, two safety pins, a
  rubber band, and a single bus ticket from another country.

![The paperweight, photographed in the same window light.](paperweight.webp)

These things are not displayed. They are not catalogued. The librarians
have, between them, agreed to let them accumulate. Once or twice a year
somebody dusts the sill, picks up the items individually, looks at each one
for a moment, and puts it back roughly where it was. Nothing has ever been
removed.

I have been coming to this library for nine years. In that time, two new
things have been added: the bus ticket and the bronze tortoise. Both
appeared on the same day, about three years ago, with no announcement and
no explanation. I have never asked who put them there. It would feel
slightly impolite.

The light comes in across the sill at a low angle for about an hour each
afternoon. For that hour the paperweight catches the sun and the daisy
inside it glows. I usually try, on days when I am at the library at the
right time, to be sitting at the desk that has the best view of it.
