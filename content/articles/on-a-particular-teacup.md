I have been drinking out of the same teacup, more or less every morning,
for eleven years. It was given to me by an aunt who is no longer here. It
is white porcelain, very thin, with a small hairline crack running from
the rim about a third of the way down one side. It came with a saucer that
I no longer have - it broke in the third year of ownership - and a lid
that I have never used.

This is, in effect, a review of one specific teacup. Your teacup is not
this teacup. Your aunt was not my aunt. Take the rating below with that
understanding.

## At a glance

| Property              | Value                                      |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Material              | Bone china, very thin                      |
| Capacity              | Approximately 180ml                        |
| Heat retention        | Poor (this is, in this case, a feature)    |
| Lip comfort           | Excellent                                  |
| Dishwasher safe       | The label says yes; I would not risk it    |
| Replaceable           | No                                         |
| Score                 | Five stars out of five                     |

The poor heat retention means the tea cools to drinking temperature within
about three minutes. This means I drink the cup of tea I have made, when
I have made it, rather than coming back to it half an hour later to find
it cold and undrinkable. The thinness of the porcelain means the cup
itself feels almost weightless. The hairline crack does not leak. The
absence of a saucer is a small daily inconvenience that I have stopped
noticing.

I will not buy another teacup. When this teacup eventually breaks - and
I expect it will, in some careless morning, in my hand or somebody else's
- I will probably not replace it at all. There are enough other cups in
the house. None of them is this cup.
