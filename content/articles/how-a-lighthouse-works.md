A lighthouse, when you stop to think about it, is really a clock. It tells
sailors not just where they are but, more usefully, *which* light they are
looking at. Every lighthouse on every coast in the world has a unique
signature - a pattern of flashes and pauses, sometimes broken by a longer
beam, sometimes a steady glow. Mariners learn the patterns the way they once
learned the shapes of constellations. From a small boat at four in the
morning, in the kind of weather where the sky and the sea are the same
colour, a lighthouse identifies itself in something close to Morse.

## The Fresnel question

For most of human history, putting a useful light on top of a tower was a
very inefficient business. A bonfire on a rock can be seen for a few miles in
clear weather, but a few miles is not enough. Oil lamps with reflectors did
better, but the reflector wastes most of the light by sending it back through
the lamp itself. The breakthrough was a French civil engineer named Augustin
Fresnel, who realised in the 1820s that a lens did not need to be one solid
block of glass. You could carve concentric rings out of a thicker lens,
discard the bulk between them, and end up with a flat-ish disk that bent
light just as effectively as the original. Stack a series of these rings into
a beehive and you had something that could take a single oil flame and throw
it twenty miles.

A first-order Fresnel lens stands taller than a person. It looks, in
photographs, like a mechanical hive built out of glass and brass. It is
heavy. The largest of them weighed more than a small car. Once a lens like
this was installed in a tower, it tended to stay installed - many of them
have been in continuous service for more than a century, occasionally
disassembled, cleaned, and reassembled in exactly the configuration the
keeper found them.

## The flash pattern

Here is the part that surprised me when I first learned it. The light inside
a lighthouse is, in most cases, not blinking. It is on, continuously, like a
bedside lamp. What blinks is the lens. The whole assembly of brass and glass
floats on a mercury bath - a shallow trough of liquid metal that lets the
several-tonne lens turn with almost no friction - and a clockwork mechanism,
wound by hand once or twice a day, drives it slowly around. The "flash" you
see from the sea is the moment when one of the lens's segments is pointed
directly at you. The "dark" is when it is pointed somewhere else.

The pattern of flashes is the lighthouse's name in the language of charts.
Three quick flashes, four seconds dark, repeat: that is one lighthouse.
Single flash every ten seconds: that is another. A "group flash four" might
be one lighthouse on the western approach to a strait; a "group flash three
white, one red" might be the next one up the coast. Open any nautical chart
and the lighthouses are listed by these signatures, the way other places are
listed by latitude and longitude.

This means the lighthouse is doing two things at once. It is being a light
- a thing visible at a great distance, in foul weather, when no other
landmark is. And it is being a clock - a mechanism so reliable that a sailor
can pull out a stopwatch, count the flashes, and know with certainty which
piece of coast the light belongs to.

## Keepers

Until quite recently, every lighthouse had at least one keeper - usually two
or three, working in shifts. The job was, by most accounts, both extremely
boring and extremely demanding. The keeper wound the clockwork. The keeper
filled the lamp. The keeper polished the brass and the glass, every day,
because soot accumulated and obscured the beam. The keeper kept the log,
which recorded the weather and the passing ships and any unusual events. The
keeper, in storms, often saved lives.

There is a romance about lighthouse keepers that is mostly imaginary. Most
of them were not solitary poets. Most of them were practical people doing a
job that had to be done, on rocks they did not always like, alongside
colleagues they did not always like either. The logs are full of
disagreements about who was supposed to fill the oil reservoir on Tuesday.
But the romance has stuck, partly because of nineteenth-century novels and
partly because the work itself - keeping a single light burning through the
night, on a rock, for nobody in particular - is a useful image of what it
means to do something carefully.

## Automation

Almost every lighthouse in the world is now automated. The lamps are
electric, often LED. The lenses, in many cases, are smaller and lighter than
the historic Fresnels, which have been removed and put in maritime museums.
The flash patterns are produced by computers rather than by the slow
revolution of clockwork. The keepers are gone. A single technician now
visits a string of lighthouses on a rotation, sometimes by helicopter,
checking solar panels and replacing batteries.

The lights themselves are still doing the same job. They identify themselves
to sailors, in the same coded patterns, on the same nautical charts. They
are still, in a real and useful sense, clocks. But they no longer require
anyone to wind them.

I find this both reassuring and slightly sad. A lighthouse is, after all, a
machine designed to be paid attention to. It is built to be seen by someone
who is alone at night and worried. The fact that nobody now climbs the
spiral stairs to wind the clockwork - that the thing runs itself, on a solar
panel, talking to a satellite - is an engineering triumph. It is also a kind
of loneliness on the lighthouse's behalf.

The next time you find yourself near a coast at dusk, watch for the lights
to come on. They will, in a sequence you cannot quite predict, all begin
flashing at once, each in its own pattern. Somewhere out at sea, a sailor
will be checking them off. It is, when you think about it, a very old
conversation.
