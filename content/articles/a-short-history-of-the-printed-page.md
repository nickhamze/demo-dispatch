Before the printed page there was the manuscript page, and before the
manuscript page there was the scroll, and before the scroll there was the
clay tablet. Each of these is, at root, the same problem with a different
solution: how do you store words such that another person, somewhere else,
can read them later.

Each solution constrains what the words can do. The clay tablet is good for
short, durable, official communication - tax records, legal codes, the kind
of message that benefits from being literally set in stone. It is bad for a
long argument. The scroll is better for a long argument but terrible for
finding a specific passage; you have to roll the whole thing forward until
you arrive at it. The codex - what we would now just call a book, with a
spine and pages you turn - is the format that makes random access
practical, which is why it became, and remains, the dominant form.

## Type before the press

The cleverness of moveable type, when it arrived in Europe in the 1450s,
was not the type itself. The Chinese had been using moveable type for four
centuries. The cleverness was the alloy. Gutenberg's contribution was a
specific, repeatable mixture of lead, tin, and antimony that cooled with
exactly the right combination of properties: it was hard enough to take an
inked impression thousands of times without deforming, soft enough to cast
into a precise mould, and shrunk in a predictable way as it cooled, so the
finished letter was the size you intended.

Without that alloy, moveable type was a craft project. With it, you had an
industry.

## Paper sizes

The standard paper sizes we now use - A4, A3, A5, and so on - were
formalised in Germany in the 1920s but the principle is much older. They are
all related by a single ratio: the long side of any sheet, divided by the
short side, equals the square root of two. This means a sheet folded in half
yields two sheets that are exactly the same proportion as the original. You
can cut paper without waste. You can scale a layout from one size to another
without distorting it.

It is, when you stop to look, a beautiful piece of design. It is also one of
the only pieces of standardised infrastructure that the United States
declined to adopt - American paper sizes are unrelated to any clean
mathematical proportion and require their own separate scaling tables in
every piece of design software.

## After the press

For five hundred years after Gutenberg, the page was set in metal type. The
typesetter stood at a case of small lead letters, picking them out one at a
time, assembling each line by hand into a metal frame. The frame was inked,
pressed against paper, and the page was printed. To change a typo, you
unlocked the frame, picked out the wrong letter, found the right one, and
locked the frame again.

In the 1880s a machine called the Linotype let an operator type the words
on a keyboard and got the machine to cast a fresh slug of type for each
line. This was much faster but it meant that to fix a typo you had to recast
the entire line. Newspapers ran on Linotype machines well into the 1980s.

In the 1960s phototypesetting did away with metal entirely. Letters were
exposed onto film and the film was then used to make printing plates. By
the late 1980s, phototypesetting had in turn been replaced by
PostScript-driven imagesetters, which were eventually replaced by
direct-to-plate systems, which now coexist with the entirely paperless
workflows of digital publishing.

Each of these transitions was experienced, by the people doing the work, as
the loss of a craft. Each of them was, on net, an improvement.

## What the page is now

The page is now mostly virtual. You are reading this on a screen that does
not have pages. The line you are reading does not have a fixed length, a
fixed typeface, or a fixed colour; the rendering changes if you resize your
window. The conventions of the printed page - the title at the top, the
paragraph indent, the line spacing - are still here, but they are
suggestions now rather than constraints. The page itself has been
disassembled into a stream of text and a set of style rules, and
reassembled, in front of you, in real time.

This is, in its way, another revolution comparable to Gutenberg's. We have
not yet finished working out what it is for. We are, mostly, still using the
new medium to do what the old medium did. Paragraphs. Headings. Blockquotes.
Numbered lists.

The clay tablets are still legible four thousand years later. Whether the
glowing rectangles will be remembered as a stage in the evolution of the
page, or as the format that finally retired it, is something nobody alive
today is in a position to know.
