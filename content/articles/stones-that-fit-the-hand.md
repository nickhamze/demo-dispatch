There is a particular kind of stone, found on most river banks in most
parts of the world, that fits exactly into a closed adult hand. It is
heavier than you expect for its size. It is smooth in a way that no
manufactured object is smooth - smoothed not by sandpaper but by tens of
thousands of years of being moved very slowly, by water, against other
similar stones. It is usually a quiet colour: grey, or pale brown, or a
muted green.

I have a small collection of these stones on a shelf in my kitchen. None of
them was acquired with intent. Each was picked up on a walk, carried in a
pocket for the rest of the day, and set down on the shelf in the evening
because I could not think of a reason to throw it away. There are now,
according to a quick count, eleven of them.

The first time I noticed I was doing this was about six years ago. The
collection at that point was four stones, on the windowsill of a kitchen
in another flat. Moving house, I packed them carefully, one at a time, in
a small cardboard box. The movers did not ask. The stones travelled
across town in the cab of the van, on the dashboard, and were set out on
the new windowsill before any of the books were unpacked.

I am not sure why I keep them. They are, honestly, just stones. They will
outlast everything else in the kitchen, including the kitchen, and one
day somebody will find them and not know whose they were or why they were
on a shelf, and they will throw the stones away or, if they are kind,
take them out to a river and put them back.

It will, in either case, be the right thing to do.
