The light in a small harbor at five o'clock in the afternoon, at this time
of year, is approximately the colour of the inside of a brass instrument.
It is not yellow. It is not orange. It is the particular metallic colour
that brass takes on when somebody has not polished it for a long time but
also has not let it tarnish.

![A sailboat at anchor in the foreground.](sailboat.webp)

The light comes in low across the water and turns every white surface -
the white of a sail, the white of a railing, the white of a sleeping
seagull - into the same brass colour. The hulls of the moored boats, which
are mostly painted some shade of cream or ivory, also become brass. The
water itself takes on the colour of the harbor floor, which here is
sand, which means the water becomes a brass-coloured mirror with the
sailboats reflected in it.

![The harbor light coming on at the end of the breakwater.](lighthouse.webp)

At about ten past five, the harbor light at the end of the breakwater
comes on. It is a small, white, unimpressive light, much less interesting
than a real lighthouse, but for the next twenty minutes it is competing
with the sunset, and it loses. By half past five the sun is below the
horizon, the brass colour drains out of everything, and the harbor light
suddenly becomes the brightest thing in the scene. The boats turn into
silhouettes. The water goes black.

Five minutes after that the second light comes on - a red light, on the
other side of the harbor entrance - and the harbor is once again, in the
old language, "open." The boats can come in.

I am sitting on a bench. I have been sitting on this bench for about
forty-five minutes. None of the boats have come in.

This is, in my experience, often how it goes.
